## Abstract
Multimodal AI has demonstrated superior performance over unimodal approaches by leveraging diverse data 
sources for more comprehensive analysis. However, applying this effectiveness in healthcare is challenging due 
to the limited availability of public datasets. Federated learning presents an exciting solution, allowing the use 
of extensive databases from hospitals and health centers without centralizing sensitive data, thus maintaining 
privacy and security. Yet, research in multimodal federated learning, particularly in scenarios with missing 
modalities—a common issue in healthcare datasets—remains scarce, highlighting a critical area for future 
exploration. Toward this, we propose a novel method for multimodal federated learning with missing 
modalities. Our contribution lies in a novel cross-modal data augmentation by retrieval, leveraging the small 
publicly available dataset to fill the missing modalities in the clients. Our method learns the parameters in a 
federated manner, ensuring privacy protection and improving performance in multiple challenging multimodal 
benchmarks in the medical domain, surpassing several competitive baselines. Code Available:

## Objectives
The work aims to (1) address the scarcity of multimodal federated learning methods that can handle missing 
modalities, (2) develop a privacy-preserving cross-modal augmentation strategy using a small public dataset, 
and (3) demonstrate superior performance over existing baselines on medical multimodal benchmarks.
## Methods
CAR-MFL operates in a standard federated learning loop where each client trains a shared model composed of 
modality-specific encoders (ResNet-50 for images, BERT-base for text), a concatenation-based fusion 
module, and a classifier head. For unimodal clients, the method retrieves the top-k visually similar images from 
the public pool using L2 distance on encoder features, refines the selection by Jaccard similarity of label sets, and 
pairs the retrieved text modality with the local image to create an augmented multimodal sample. The local loss 
is then computed on these augmented pairs. To mitigate noise from imperfect retrievals, encoder-specific 
aggregation weights are down-scaled by a hyper-parameter α=0.3 before softmax normalization.
Cross-modal augmentation: retrieve and pair missing modality samples from the public dataset for each 
unimodal client during training.
Baseline comparisons: (i) mFedAvg (standard FedAvg), (ii) mFedAvgP (mFedAvg with a public data “virtual 
client”), both with and without public data, and (iii) CreamFL (state-of-the-art MFL method).
Training setups: homogeneous (identical data distribution) and heterogeneous (non-identical distribution) 
client partitions, varying the number of multimodal versus unimodal clients.
Evaluation metrics: Area Under the Curve (AUC) for overall performance and specific analysis on rare classes; 
also examined modality-bias via weight distribution in the classification layer.
## Results
Across both homogeneous and heterogeneous federated configurations, CAR-MFL achieves higher macro-AUC 
scores than zero-filling, generative, and other baseline methods, demonstrating consistent quantitative and 
qualitative gains on the multimodal test set. The reported improvements are obtained after 30 communication 
rounds with 3 local epochs per round, using three random seeds for robustness.
CAR-MFL consistently achieved higher AUC than all baselines across every client configuration.
In the homogeneous setup, with only 2 multimodal clients, CAR-MFL surpassed mFedAvgP that used 6 
multimodal clients.
In the heterogeneous setup, CAR-MFL outperformed mFedAvgP by 4%.
Even when the public dataset was reduced to 250 patients, CAR-MFL still beat mFedAvgP by 6% in AUC.
Analysis of rare classes showed that CAR-MFL retained performance as the proportion of multimodal clients 
decreased, whereas mFedAvgP suffered a marked drop.
Modality-bias assessment revealed that mFedAvgP concentrated weights on the dominant modality, while 
CAR-MFL achieved near-equal weight distribution across image and text features.
## Conclusions
Cross-modal retrieval-based augmentation enables effective multimodal federated learning despite missing 
modalities, leveraging a modest public dataset while maintaining privacy through client-side processing and 
k-anonymity. The approach outperforms existing strategies and establishes a new baseline for multimodal 
federated medical AI.

## Study limitations
The framework depends on the availability of a representative public multimodal dataset; retrieval quality may 
be low early in training, requiring label-refinement and weight re-adjustment to avoid noise. Additionally, the 
method currently handles only image and text modalities and assumes a fixed encoder architecture.
## Future work
Planned extensions include scaling the retrieval pool to larger, more diverse public datasets, incorporating 
additional modalities such as omics or pathology reports, and exploring adaptive retrieval strategies that reduce 
reliance on label refinement. Further research will also investigate stronger privacy guarantees and 
decentralized retrieval mechanisms.
## Practical applications
CAR-MFL can be deployed in hospital networks to train diagnostic models that combine imaging, textual 
reports, and potentially other clinical data without centralizing patient records, thereby enhancing disease 
detection accuracy while complying with privacy regulations.
