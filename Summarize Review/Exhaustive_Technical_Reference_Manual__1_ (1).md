1. Source and Scope: This document summarizes the CMU/CVPR Tutorial on Multimodal Machine Learning (221 slides) by Louis-Philippe Morency and Tadas Baltrusaitis, based on their survey "Multimodal Machine Learning: A Survey and Taxonomy" (arXiv:1705.09406).
2. Tutorial Structure: Organized into seven blocks covering introduction, basic ML concepts, unimodal representations, multimodal representations, RNNs/LSTMs, translation/alignment, and multimodal fusion.
3. Statistical Origin of "Multimodal": The term derives from multimodal probability distributions, which have multiple distinct peaks (modes) in their probability density function, metaphorically extended to mean multiple distinct sensory information types in ML.
4. Modality Definition: Modality refers to a type of information and/or representation format (content + format), such as vision, touch, or language, each constituting a distinct channel of communication.
5. Medium Definition: Medium is the means or channel through which information is delivered to the interpreter's senses (e.g., television, paper, air for sound waves)—infrastructure distinct from modality content.
6. Multimedia vs. Multimodal Paradigm Shift: Multimedia (1990s–2000s) focused on storage/retrieval/delivery of heterogeneous media formats, while multimodal research (2010s onward) focuses on joint representation learning, cross-modal alignment, and fusion for prediction tasks.
7. Complete Modality Enumeration: The tutorial lists natural language, visual, auditory, haptics/touch, smell/taste/self-motion, physiological signals (ECG, skin conductance), and other modalities (infrared, depth, fMRI).
8. Multimodal Communicative Behaviors: Human communication includes verbal behaviors (lexicon, syntax, pragmatics), vocal behaviors (prosody, vocal expressions), and visual behaviors (gestures, body language, eye contact, facial expressions via FACS).
9. Cross-Community Nature: Multimodal ML draws from psychology, medicine, speech, vision, language, multimedia, robotics, and machine learning communities, explaining its historically fragmented terminology.
10. Era 1 — Behavioral Era (1970s–late 1980s): Dominated by psychological/behavioral theory; key contributions include Arnold Lazarus's Multimodal Behavior Therapy and David McNeill's work establishing gesture as integral to speech cognition.
11. McGurk Effect (1976): A landmark finding showing that conflicting audio ("ba") and visual lip ("ga") cues fuse preconsciously into a new percept ("da"), motivating Audio-Visual Speech Recognition (AVSR) research.
12. Era 2 — Computational Era (late 1980s–2000): Engineering era featuring AVSR systems (first system in 1986), multimodal/multisensory interfaces (Glove-Talk, Affective Computing by Rosalind Picard), and multimedia computing (Informedia Digital Video Library).
13. Computational Models of Era 2: Hidden Markov Models (1960s) and their extensions—Factorial HMMs (1996, multiple independent hidden chains) and Coupled HMMs (1997, cross-chain dependencies)—alongside early neural network advances (backpropagation, CNNs).
14. Era 3 — Interaction Era (2000s): Focused on naturalistic human-human/human-computer interaction via major projects (AMI, CHIL, CALO—the precursor to Siri—and SSP), plus multimedia retrieval competitions (TRECVid).
15. Computational Models of Era 3: Dynamic Bayesian Networks and discriminative sequential models (Conditional Random Fields, Latent-Dynamic CRFs) enabled more flexible multimodal temporal modeling than generative HMMs.
16. Era 4 — Deep Learning Era (2010s–present): The tutorial's primary focus, marked by multimodal deep learning (ICML 2011), Deep Boltzmann Machines (NIPS 2012), and attention-based image captioning (ICML 2015), enabled by large datasets, GPUs, pretrained CNN features, and word embeddings.
17. Real-World Application Taxonomy: Covers affect recognition (emotion, persuasion, personality), media description (captioning, VQA), event recognition (action recognition/segmentation), and multimedia information retrieval.
18. Five Core Technical Challenges: Representation Learning, Alignment, Fusion, Translation, and Co-Learning are non-exclusive and interdependent challenges that any multimodal system may need to address simultaneously.
19. Representation Learning Definition: Learning to represent/summarize multimodal data while exploiting complementarity and redundancy across modalities, divided into Joint and Coordinated representation paradigms.
20. Joint Representations: Map all modalities into a single shared latent space via unimodal encoders; key architectures include Bimodal Deep Belief Networks (Ngiam et al. 2011) and Multimodal Deep Boltzmann Machines (Srivastava & Salakhutdinov 2012).
21. Multimodal Vector Arithmetic: Once embedded in a joint space, image and word vectors support algebraically meaningful operations (Kiros et al. 2014), analogous to word2vec's semantic analogies.
22. Tensor Fusion Network (TFN): Explicitly models unimodal, bimodal, and trimodal interactions via outer-product (tensor) operations across modality representations, improving multimodal sentiment analysis on the MOSI dataset (Zadeh, Jones & Morency, EMNLP 2017).
23. Higher-Order Interaction Motivation: Examples show that single-modality cues (text alone) can be ambiguous, while bimodal or trimodal combinations (text+smile+loud voice) resolve sentiment, motivating explicit interaction modeling.
24. Coordinated Representations: Each modality keeps its own representation space, structurally aligned via similarity/correlation constraints; matched instances map to nearby points while unmatched instances map far apart (Huang et al. 2013).
25. Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA): Classical algorithm learning linear, mutually orthogonal projection pairs that maximize cross-view correlation, equivalent under normalization to minimizing RMSE reconstruction loss.
26. Deep CCA (DCCA) and DCCAE: DCCA (Andrew et al. 2013) replaces CCA's linear projections with deep non-linear networks while preserving correlation maximization, orthogonality, and unit-variance constraints; DCCAE (Wang et al. 2015) adds a reconstruction objective traded off against correlation via a hyperparameter.
27. Alignment Definition: Identifying direct relations between sub-elements across different modalities, split into Explicit Alignment (directly finding correspondences) and Implicit Alignment (latent alignment used to solve a different downstream problem).
28. Fusion Definition: Joining information from two or more modalities to perform a prediction task, encompassing model-agnostic (early/late/hybrid) and model-based (kernel, graphical, neural network) approaches.
29. Translation Definition: Converting data from one modality to another, often open-ended/subjective, via example-based (retrieval/combination) or model-based (grammar-based, encoder-decoder, online prediction) approaches; challenges include representation mismatch and subjective evaluation.
30. Co-Learning Definition: Transferring knowledge (representations or predictive models) between modalities, covering Parallel Data (co-training, transfer learning), Non-Parallel Data (zero-shot learning, concept grounding), and Hybrid Data (bridging via a third modality).
31. Full Taxonomy Structure: The survey's taxonomy subdivides each of the five challenges (Representation, Translation, Alignment, Fusion, Co-learning) into detailed methodological subcategories (e.g., Fusion splits into model-agnostic early/late/hybrid and model-based kernel/graphical/neural approaches).
32. Linear Classification Basics: A linear score function f(x;W,b) = Wx+b defines a planar decision boundary; bias can be absorbed into the weight matrix via input vector augmentation.
33. Cross-Entropy and Hinge Loss: Cross-entropy loss minimizes negative log-likelihood of the correct class via the softmax function; hinge (max-margin/SVM) loss penalizes incorrect classes scoring within a margin Δ of the correct class.
34. Neural Network Basics: Artificial neurons compute y = f(Wx+b); common activation functions are tanh, sigmoid, linear, and ReLU, with ReLU favored for faster training and sparsity-inducing properties.
35. Optimization Fundamentals: Gradient descent updates parameters via θ = θ - ε∇L; backpropagation computes gradients via the chain rule through forward and backward passes; extensions include momentum, AdaGrad, RMSProp, and quasi-Newton methods (BFGS, LBFGS).
36. Language Representations—Word-Level and Document-Level: Word-level tasks use one-hot vectors; document-level tasks (e.g., sentiment analysis) use bag-of-words vectors counting word frequency.
37. Word Embeddings (word2vec): Based on the distributional hypothesis (words in similar contexts have similar meaning), word2vec predicts surrounding words from a target word via a shallow network, producing dense embeddings that capture semantic similarity unlike orthogonal one-hot vectors.
38. Vector Space Semantic/Syntactic Regularities: Word embedding spaces support algebraic analogy operations (e.g., king − man + woman ≈ queen), trained at scale on corpora like Google News (300+ billion words).
39. Classical Visual Descriptors: Pre-deep-learning visual modality relied on hand-crafted features including HoG, Local Binary Patterns, SIFT, optical flow, and Gabor jets.
40. CNN Motivation over MLP: MLPs applied to images require excessive parameters and fail to exploit translation invariance; CNNs address this via convolution (sparse, weight-shared local connections) and pooling (differentiable sub-sampling approximating max).
41. Acoustic Signal Representation: Audio is digitized at 8–96 kHz sampling rates with 8/16/24-bit depth, segmented into 20ms frames with 10ms (50%) overlap, and represented as spectrograms for tasks like emotion recognition and speech recognition.
42. Autoencoders: Self-encoding networks with an encoder (compressing input to hidden representation) and decoder (reconstructing input), often using tied weights (W*=W^T); denoising autoencoders add input noise to learn more robust representations; stacked autoencoders are trained greedily layer-by-layer then fine-tuned end-to-end.
43. Deep Multimodal Boltzmann Machines: Generative models with separate per-modality towers (e.g., Depth, Video, Verbal) feeding a shared joint representation, trained via variational approaches, enabling cross-modal sampling and reconstruction (Srivastava & Salakhutdinov 2012/2014).
44. Deep Multimodal Autoencoders: Bimodal autoencoders for audio-visual speech recognition (Ngiam et al. 2011) trained with cross-modal reconstruction (reconstructing one modality from the other) to force a genuinely shared representation.
45. RNN Fundamentals: Unlike feedforward networks, RNNs propagate a hidden state across time steps (h_t = tanh(Ux_t + Wh_{t-1})) with shared parameters across all time steps, trained via Backpropagation Through Time (BPTT).
46. Vanishing/Exploding Gradient Problem: In RNNs, gradient influence decays or blows up exponentially through recurrent connections depending on the largest eigenvalue of the recurrent weight matrix, motivating LSTM development.
47. LSTM Architecture: Solves vanishing gradients via a memory cell with self-loop, plus input, output (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber 1997), and forget gates (Gers et al. 2000); equations combine gated candidate content, retained memory, and gated output via element-wise operations.
48. LSTM Variants: Bidirectional LSTMs process sequences in both directions and concatenate hidden states; Deep LSTMs stack multiple LSTM layers, each layer's output feeding the next.
49. Cross-Media Retrieval (Translation): A bounded, objectively evaluable translation task using representations like CCA, coordinated, or joint embeddings, plus hashing, to retrieve closest matching items across modalities.
50. Image Captioning Encoder-Decoder: Couples a CNN visual encoder (fixed-dimensional image vector) with an LSTM language decoder generating captions word-by-word, trained end-to-end with cross-entropy loss (Vinyals et al., "Show and Tell," 2015).
51. Visual Question Answering (VQA): Created partly to address evaluation problems in image captioning; given an image and question, the system must produce an answer.
52. Translation Evaluation: "Unbounded" translation outputs are hard to evaluate automatically; standard machine translation metrics (BLEU, ROUGE, CIDEr, METEOR) are used as proxies for human judgment.
53. Dynamic Time Warping (DTW): Aligns two unimodal temporal signals by finding the minimum-cost monotonic, continuous index-matching path via dynamic programming, subject to boundary, warping-window, and slope constraints; limited by computational complexity and inability to handle multimodal data directly.
54. Canonical Time Warping (CTW): Combines DTW with CCA to enable cross-modal/cross-view temporal alignment by jointly learning spatial projections (U,V) and temporal alignment matrices (W_x, W_y) (Zhou & De la Torre 2009).
55. Generalized Time Warping (GTW) and Deep CTW (DCTW): GTW generalizes CTW to multiple sequences of different modalities (Zhou & De la Torre 2016); DCTW further generalizes to non-linear alignment functions via deep networks, solved iteratively by alternating eigendecomposition and gradient descent (Trigeorgis et al. 2016).
56. Attention for Machine Translation: Bahdanau et al. (2015) introduced computing a context vector as a weighted sum over all encoder hidden states (rather than just the final state), creating a "soft alignment" between source and target tokens at each decoding step.
57. Attention for Image Captioning: "Show, Attend and Tell" (Xu et al. 2015) distributes attention weights over spatial CNN feature locations at each decoding step, producing a dynamically computed visual context for word generation.
58. Temporal Attention-Gated Model (TAGM): Uses a recurrent attention gate that either copies the previous hidden state (ignoring noisy input) or processes the current input normally, outperforming RNN/GRU/LSTM baselines on the CCV video classification dataset (Pei et al., CVPR 2017).
59. Fusion Definition and Domains: Joining multimodal information for prediction, applied to AVSR, multimedia event detection, and multimodal emotion recognition, split into model-free and model-based categories.
60. Early Fusion: Concatenates all modality features into a single vector before classification; easy to implement and captures cross-modal dependencies, but produces high-dimensional features and requires all modalities present at train/test time.
61. Late Fusion: Trains separate unimodal classifiers and combines their predictions (voting, weighted sum, or learned fusion); handles missing modalities at test time but misses low-level cross-modal interactions and creates a decision-level information bottleneck.
62. Hybrid Fusion: Combines early-fused and individually-trained unimodal classifiers, with a final fusion mechanism integrating both types of predictions.
63. Multiple Kernel Learning: Generalizes SVMs by learning non-negative weights for a combination of per-modality kernels, requiring little adaptation between unimodal and multimodal settings (Lanckriet 2004).
64. Multi-View Hidden Conditional Random Field (HCRF): Models both modality-private structure (within-modality grouping) and modality-shared structure (cross-modal synchrony) via hidden variables, approximated with loopy belief propagation (Song, Morency & Davis, CVPR 2012).
65. Multi-View LSTM (MV-LSTM): Extends LSTM with per-view gated inputs and memory cells, supporting four topologies (view-specific, fully-connected, coupled, hybrid) controlling how much memory derives from the current vs. other views (Shyam, Morency et al., ECCV 2016).
66. Memory Fusion Network: Attaches an external memory module that accumulates multimodal information over a sequence without modifying the source network's structure, used for multi-view sequential learning (Zadeh et al., AAAI 2018).
67. System Architecture Pipeline: A generalized audio-visual pipeline includes (1) raw audio/visual stream preprocessing, (2) unimodal LSTM/CNN encoders, (3) explicit (DTW/CTW) or implicit (attention-based) alignment, (4) gated fusion combining aligned representations, and (5) a sequence- or frame-level prediction head, trained end-to-end via cross-entropy or Euclidean loss.
68. No-Token-Suppression Principle: In the gated fusion block, every audio and visual hidden state contributes to the fused representation via attention weights and gate values, ensuring full gradient flow back through both encoder streams during training.
69. Architecture Reference Map: Key architectures span Bimodal DBN (AVSR), Multimodal DBM (image tagging/retrieval), DCCA/DCCAE (coordinated representation), Tensor Fusion Network (sentiment analysis), Canonical/Generalized/Deep Canonical Time Warping (alignment), attention models (machine translation, captioning), TAGM (sequence classification), and Multi-View HCRF/LSTM/Memory Fusion Network (sequential fusion).
